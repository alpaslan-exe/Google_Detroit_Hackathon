# StaySignal

**Google × CSG × T4SG Hackathon · April 10, 2026**

> Detroit has over 120,000 rental properties, but only about 8% are fully compliant with the city's 2024 Rental Ordinance. The data exists — crime, blight, registrations — but it's scattered across three government portals that no tenant has time to query.
>
> StaySignal is what happens when you put that data in front of the person who actually needs it, at the moment they're about to sign a lease.

---

## What it does

A renter types any Detroit address. In under two seconds they get back:

1. **A 0–100 safety score** with a color-coded label (LOW / MODERATE / HIGH RISK)
2. **A component breakdown** so they can see *why* — recent crime nearby, active blight tickets, whether the property is registered with BSEED
3. **A plain-English explanation** generated by an LLM that names the most important issue first
4. **A concrete next action** — sign, negotiate, or report — with the legal mechanism to act on it
5. **For high-risk properties: a draft BSEED complaint letter** the tenant can send immediately, citing the right city ordinance and the right department contact
6. **A follow-up chat** — the tenant can ask "I already signed the lease, what now?" or "Can I withhold rent legally?" and get Detroit-specific guidance with the property's full context already loaded

The whole interaction takes less than 30 seconds. No account, no payment, no jargon.

## Why it's innovative

**1. It's a decision tool, not a dashboard.**
Most civic-tech projects translate data into charts. StaySignal translates data into one of three actions: *sign*, *negotiate*, or *report*. The score is a means to that decision, not the end.

**2. It composes three datasets that have never been joined for tenants.**
Detroit's police crime data, BSEED rental registrations, and DAH blight violations are all public, but no city tool combines them — and certainly not with a tenant as the user. The combination is what makes the score meaningful: a registered property in a high-crime area is a *different* recommendation than an unregistered property in a quiet block.

**3. The AI is doing legal reasoning, not just summarization.**
The model is prompted as a Detroit tenant-rights advisor. For high-risk lookups, it drafts a ready-to-send BSEED complaint letter that cites the correct ordinance and the correct city department. Templates can't do this — every property needs its own facts and its own framing.

**4. Conversation, not a one-shot answer.**
After the score loads, a chat panel keeps the property's full context loaded into every turn. A tenant can ask follow-up legal questions ("can I get my deposit back?" / "what happens after I file a complaint?") and get answers grounded in the actual data they just looked up. This is the part that makes AI irreplaceable here — a static template can't hold a conversation about the user's specific situation.

**5. The score is calibrated to Detroit, not to a generic risk model.**
The thresholds were tuned against real residential addresses across the city until the three risk tiers actually separated meaningful neighborhood categories. A "HIGH RISK" label means something specific — it's not an arbitrary cutoff.

**6. It scales to any city that publishes open data.**
Chicago, Baltimore, and Cleveland all publish equivalent crime and housing datasets. The pipeline (geocode → query → compose → explain) is city-agnostic; only the calibration constants and the legal language are local. The same architecture could land in another city in days.

## Architecture at a glance

```
User types address
        │
        ▼
Frontend  ──►  /api/score_by_address
        │
        ▼
Geocoding (Nominatim, OpenStreetMap, free)
        │
        ▼
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Scoring engine — runs three independent queries:      │
│   • Crime count       — live ArcGIS, 500m / 90 days   │
│   • Blight tickets    — local CSV, 300m, active only  │
│   • Rental compliance — live ArcGIS, 100m radius      │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
        │
        ▼
compute_safety_score()  ─►  { score, label, component breakdown }
        │
        ▼
LLM advisor  ─►  plain-English explanation + decision + (if high risk)
                 ready-to-send BSEED complaint letter
        │
        ▼
JSON response  ─►  React score card + AI panel + map pin
```

**Stack:** React (Vite + Leaflet) on the frontend. Flask + pandas on the backend. All three Detroit datasets are public — no API keys, no scraping, no proprietary data.

## Scoring engine

The score is intentionally explainable, not a black box. Three independent components:

| Component       | Weight | Source                              | Reflects                  |
|-----------------|--------|-------------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Crime           | 40 pts | DPD RMS (live ArcGIS)               | Environmental risk        |
| Blight          | 30 pts | DAH violations CSV (local, filtered)| Structural / safety risk  |
| Compliance      | 30 pts | BSEED rental registry (live ArcGIS) | Legal status              |

```python
crime_score      = max(0, 40 - crime_count  * 0.15)   # softer slope than naive linear
blight_score     = max(0, 30 - blight_count * 1.00)
compliance_score = 30 if is_compliant else 0
total            = crime_score + blight_score + compliance_score

# 70+  → LOW RISK
# 45+  → MODERATE RISK
# else → HIGH RISK
```

The blight CSV is pre-filtered on startup to "active problems only" — `Responsible` disposition, last 2 years, with an unpaid balance. This drops ~97% of the 816 k raw rows down to ~27 k meaningful records, so a "blight count" reflects actual unresolved issues rather than historical noise.

Full technical reference (calibration rationale, data source URLs, known caveats, how to verify the formulas yourself) lives in [`backend/README.md`](backend/README.md).

## Data sources

All three datasets are published by the City of Detroit and require no API key:

| Data                   | Publisher                                                  | Method                  |
|------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------|
| Crime incidents        | Detroit Police Department (DPD) — RMS                      | ArcGIS REST, live query |
| Rental registrations   | BSEED — Buildings, Safety Engineering & Environmental Dept | ArcGIS REST, live query |
| Blight violations      | DAH — Department of Administrative Hearings                | Direct CSV download     |

Geocoding is handled by Nominatim (OpenStreetMap), also free.

## A note on what the score does and doesn't claim

The compliance signal is a *relative* one, not a legal verdict. A property without a nearby BSEED registration could be an unregistered rental — or it could simply not be a residential rental at all (a business, a vacant lot). StaySignal surfaces this as something to verify, not as proof of wrongdoing. The complaint letter is drafted with that caveat baked in: it asks for an inspection, not for an indictment.

This matters because the tool is meant to *inform* a tenant's decision, not to substitute for one.
